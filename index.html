A B S T R A C T

Acid stimulation within carbonate rocks represents a crucial aspect of reactive flow applications in subsurface porous media and has been widely used in carbonate reservoir development attributed to its rapid effectiveness and cost-efficiency. This study is motivated to investigate further the impact of rock heterogeneity on the acid stimulation process. The two-scale continuum model is employed for capturing mass, momentum, and energy transfer during the acid stimulation process. The core-scale model is expanded to consider the specific composition of solid minerals, including reactive minerals such as calcite and dolomite and non-reactive ones like quartz and clay. A heterogeneity generator is developed by coupling with the open-source geostatistical program to produce different heterogeneous parameters using random seeds with varying spatial correlation lengths. Sensitive cases are performed to present an investigation into the influence of mineral composition and matrix heterogeneity on the acid stimulation process. Results show that the typical results of acid stimulation are closely tied to different heterogeneity conditions. The optimum conditions for acid stimulation are sensitive to the concentration of reactive components. The optimum acid injection rate declines as dolomite content increases, while the corresponding acidizing breakthrough volume reduces with higher calcite levels. The presence of mineral layers, especially in horizontal distributions, complicates acid fluid efficiency due to the competitive reaction between calcite and dolomite. Compared to the heterogeneous distribution of minerals, matrix heterogeneity plays a critical role in acid fluid consumption efficiency. Increasing heterogeneity leads to significant reductions in breakthrough volumes, largely governed by the correlation length.

1. Introduction

Acid stimulation in carbonate rocks represents a quintessential application of reactive flow in subsurface porous media [1,2] . During acid stimulation, the acid solution is introduced into low-permeability carbonate reservoirs or zones contaminated near the wellbore during the production or injection process, all while maintaining pressures below the formation fracture threshold [3,4] . Then,the injected acid solution interacts with nearby carbonate minerals, chiefly calcite and dolomite, which leads to the competitive dissolution of the reactive minerals and subsequently forms high-conductivity pathways, commonly referred to as wormholes [5,6] . These wormholes effectively reduce the flow resistance of injected or produced fluids, enhancing and restoring the well injectability or productivity. Ultimately, acid stimulation aids in enhancing the recovery efficiency of carbonate reservoirs [7,8] .
Acid stimulation has been extensively utilized in the development of carbonate reservoirs due to its swift effectiveness and cost-efficiency. Concurrently, the hydrological, thermal, and chemical coupling processes involved in acid stimulation and the competitive dissolution leading to wormhole phenomena have also garnered significant research attention [4]. Indoor core flooding experiments provide the most intuitive visualization of the process [3]. The quintessential experimental design involves injecting acid solution at a constant rate into one end of the target core sample. The opposite end of the core maintains a consistent back pressure to sustain outflow boundary conditions while the remaining boundaries of the core remain close [9,10] . Throughout the indoor core flooding procedure, the pressure differential across the target core sample is continuously monitored as an indirect measure of the acid stimulation progress. The stop condition of the acid stimulation experiment is signified by a notable reduction in the pressure differential across the target core sample [11,12] . Utilizing micro-CT imaging on the target core sample at this juncture reveals the formation of worm-like channels within the core, connecting the inlet and outlet sections [5, 6]. This specific moment, when there is a significant drop in pressure differential, is commonly termed the breakthrough moment [3]. The internal channel structures in the core are referred to as wormholes. The volume of the consumed acid solution at this point is collected and nondimensionalized against the initial pore volume of the target core, resulting in a crucial parameter in the quantitative evaluation of acid stimulation, Pore Volume to BreakThrough (PVBT) [6,10]. Furthermore, several theoretical models have been developed to investigate and replicate the processes and results of acid stimulation flooding experiments. Notable among these are the empirical model [13,14] ,the direct numerical simulation model [15,16] ,the pore-network model [17,18] , and the two-scale continuum model [19,20] . The two-scale continuum model has demonstrated significant competitiveness in terms of computational resource efficiency and its ability to replicate the results of physical experiments [4,8] . Compared to the empirical model,the two-scale continuum model can reproduce wormhole patterns almost identical to those observed in physical experiments [4,21]. When set against the direct numerical simulation and pore-network models, the two-scale continuum model, by not explicitly tracking the micro-scale pore structure changes due to chemical reactions, conserves a significant amount of computational resources without restricting the computational domain to a very limited region [16,18] . The two-scale continuum model comprises both pore-scale and core-scale models. Initially, the core-scale model is established based on conservation laws to describe mass and momentum transfers during the acid stimulation process [19,20] . Subsequently,it is expanded to account for energy changes caused by chemical reactions [21,22] ,adjustments considering the dissolution of the solid matrix in the continuity equation leading to changes in the fluid phase mass [7,23] ,and employed a more generalized Navier-Stokes-Darcy equation to collectively describe fluid momentum transfer in both wormhole free flow regions and unstimulated porous media [4,8] . The pore-scale model serves as a constitutive equation to update rock property parameters and acid fluid transport and reaction process parameters in response to pore structure changes caused by chemical erosion of the solid matrix during acid stimulation. Moreover, the pore-scale model addresses the shortcomings of the core-scale model, especially when compared to the direct numerical simulation and pore-network models, in terms of considering microscale phenomena [4,19] .

As for the acid stimulation research, one of the most seminal and widely verified conclusions, both through physical experiments and numerical simulations, is the competitive dissolution associated with the acid solution injection rate [3,19] . By aggregating the breakthrough volume of acid solution required to penetrate target core samples at varying injection rates, it becomes evident that there invariably exists an optimum acid injection rate [5,24] . At the optimum rate,the pore volume to breakthrough is minimized. Notably, this optimum acid injection rate is consistently observed across diverse acid systems, such as hydrochloric acid [5,10] ,chelating acid [6,12] ,foamed acid [25,26] ,and various carbonate rock systems, including calcite and dolomite [27,28]. When the injection rate of the acid solution deviates from the optimum rate, there is an increased consumption of acid fluid required to breakthrough the target core sample. At higher injection rates, more wormholes are induced within the target core sample. The simultaneous development of multiple wormholes leads to increased acid fluid consumption [3,4] . Conversely,at lower injection rates,even though a single wormhole structure might develop within the target sample, the acid fluid does not efficiently propagate toward the intended core breakthrough, which results in excessive development and enlargement of the single wormhole, leading to increased acid fluid consumption and expanded wormhole diameter [19,20] . Hence,accurate prediction of the optimum acid stimulation conditions is pivotal for research into acid stimulation and its practical application. This importance has catalyzed a series of sensitive investigations. For instance, numerous experimental and numerical simulation studies have been focused on designing and optimizing various acid solution types to enhance the efficiency of acid stimulation [25,26] . Research findings demonstrate that the utilization of acid solutions with varying reactivity and diffusivity significantly impacts and modifies the optimum conditions for acid stimulation [3,4] . Acid systems with lower reactivity, such as formic acid, citric acid [3], or other similar acids like citric acid and chelating acid [29,30] ,reduce the optimum acid injection rate, simplifying the operational complexities associated with the specific application. However, a notable drawback is that these less reactive acid systems tend to increase the volume required for an acid breakthrough at the optimum injection rate, thereby escalating the costs of acid stimulation [3,4] . Another strategic approach to optimizing acid solution design focuses on altering the diffusivity of the acid fluid, with viscosity frequently serving as the primary parameter for modification. Various additives, such as surfactants, polymers, and modified shear-thinning acid solutions, can be integrated into the conventional hydrochloric acid system [31,32] . The design also links the viscosity of the acid fluid with the ionic concentration in the acid stimulation system, such as acid concentration and calcium ion concentration, culminating in the development of diversion acid systems [12,33] . Results indicate that curtailing the diffusivity of the acid fluid can concurrently reduce both the optimum acid injection rate and acid breakthrough volume. The dual benefit simplifies and economizes the acid stimulation process. Moreover, diversion acid systems can further enhance the dissolution of reservoir rocks, expanding the stimulated area [34,35] . Temperature is another paramount factor influencing the efficacy of acid solutions. Variations in the system temperature during acid stimulation substantially affect the performance of the acid fluid [30,36] . For example,a rise in system temperature intensifies the reaction between the acid fluid and the reactive minerals in carbonate rocks. The heightened reactivity efficaciously diminishes the volume of acid fluid required to breakthrough a target core at higher injection rates [21,22] .
Compared to the variations in acid solution properties, the sensitivity analysis of solid core parameters is rather limited. The limitation primarily arises due to the inherent challenges in obtaining diverse rock core samples for specific experimental setups [7,8] . Usually,geometrical dimensions of the rock core, such as diameter and length, are the most widely discussed parameters in related experimental research [37,38]. Other parameters like rock core porosity and permeability are more often explored using numerical simulations, specifically focusing on the absolute changes in values of porosity and permeability [33,39] . The motivation for this paper is to delve further into the impact of rock property heterogeneity on the acid stimulation process. A two-scale continuum model is employed for numerical simulations, capturing mass, momentum, and energy transfer during the acid stimulation process. A heterogeneity generator is developed by coupling with the open-source geostatistical program to generate random seeds with varying spatial correlation lengths, which can subsequently be utilized to produce different heterogeneous parameters for numerical calculations. The core-scale model is expanded to consider the specific composition of solid minerals, which enables an investigation into the influence of mineral composition heterogeneity, including reactive minerals such as calcite and dolomite and non-reactive ones like quartz and clay, on acid stimulation. Further details regarding the model development and implementation are elaborated in the subsequent section. The main conclusions are summarized in the final section.

2. Model description

The two-scale continuum model, including the core-scale model and pore-scale model, is employed to describe the acid stimulation process [8,19] . Compared with the previous form,this work further expands the mass conservation equation of the solid phase to consider mineral compositions. In most previous studies, the reaction mechanisms and kinetics analysis assume that the carbonate rock is limestone with single calcite as the mineral composition. The basis for such assumptions primarily lies in the fact that limestone, mainly composed of calcite, has indeed been reported in studies on carbonate rock acid stimulation. For instance, in the research conducted by Arslan, et al. [40] and Alkhaldi, et al. [41] on the dissolution reaction kinetics of limestone, the samples used are analyzed by X-ray diffraction to have a limestone composition of 99% calcite. Similarly,in the experiments conducted by Maheshwari, et al. [42] to determine wormhole morphology, limestone from Rochemont is used, and mineral analysis results showed it contains 98.7 % calcite. However,the actual mineral composition of more commonly encountered carbonate rocks does not consist of a single mineral but is diverse. In addition, the open-source geostatistical program, Geo-statistical Software Library, and its related software (GSLIB) are employed to generate random seeds with varying spatial correlation lengths [43,44] . These seeds are subsequently utilized to produce different heterogeneous parameters characterized by specific features.

2.3. Model implementation

Simulation cases are conducted on a 2D experiment-scale core sample with a length of 15cm and a width of 4cm [3]. Boundary conditions are determined referring to experimental performance. During the acid stimulation process, the acid solution is injected from the inlet of the target core sample with a constant injection rate, temperature, and concentration [5,29]. The outlet pressure remains a constant of 7 Mpa to maintain a single-phase system. Transverse boundaries remain closed. Initially, the core sample is saturated with water. The heterogeneous distribution is realized through an open-source geo-statistical program, GSLIB, using Gaussian Sequential Algorithms. Random seeds satisfying the normal distribution can be generated with different spatial correlation lengths further for the generation of other heterogeneous parameters, such as porosity, permeability, and reactive components [43,44] . Spatial autocorrelation refers to the correlation between the attribute values at a location in space and the values of its surrounding locations, with the correlation diminishing as distance increases [47]. For underground spaces, the correlation characteristic is particularly significant because the formation and evolution of underground media are influenced by long-term geological processes [48]. Sedimentary rocks are formed through the accumulation of sediments, processes that exhibit a certain degree of spatial continuity and consistency, leading to underground media at proximate locations often having similar physical and chemical properties [49,50] . Moreover,the movement of groundwater and geological fluids, as well as subsequent diagenesis, can also affect the physical and chemical properties of underground media [51,52] . These processes often proceed in specific directions, causing properties along the flow path to exhibit spatial autocorrelation. Therefore, the physical properties of underground media during their formation are subject to the combined effects of the above factors, thereby displaying regularity and autocorrelation in space. For heterogeneous porosity and reactive component generation, random seeds are normalized and used with the heterogeneity magnitude to produce parameters that satisfy a specific range distribution. Permeability heterogeneity is described by the Dykstra-Parsons coefficient (VDP) by changing the log deviation value of permeability distribution. Benchmark cases are designed with a correlation length of 0.01 , permeability VDP of 0.4,and porosity heterogeneity magnitude of 0.15 . The finite volume method is employed to solve the two-scale continuum model based on the Matlab platform. The sequential decoupling algorithm is used to address the strongly coupled hydrological-thermal-chemical processes during the acid stimulation process. Specifically, before the model is numerically discretized for solving, all input parameters of the model are initialized. This includes rock physical parameters such as porosity, permeability, and pore radius, as well as acid fluid physical parameters like acid diffusion rate and chemical reaction rate, along with the model sought variables such as pressure, velocity, acid concentration, and temperature. Subsequently, at each time step, the continuity equation for the fluid phase and the fluid phase momentum conservation equation, the Navier-Stokes-Darcy equation, are first solved together to determine the pressure distribution and velocity distribution of the fluid phase. The velocity of the fluid phase obtained from the solution is then explicitly used to determine the strength of the acid solution convection in the acid solution mass conservation equation, thereby determining the bulk concentration of the acid solution. Similarly, the determined fluid phase velocity is also used in the energy conservation equation to solve for the system temperature. Unlike the bulk phase chemical reaction process, the chemical reaction in acid stimulation involves heterogeneous chemical reactions, meaning the actual chemical reaction rate is determined by the acid concentration on the rock surface. Therefore, the mass transfer equation for the acid solution requires additional solving to determine the concentration of the acid solution at the rock surface. The determined concentration of the acid solution is used along with the system temperature to update the dynamics and thermodynamic rates within the entire chemical reaction system. In turn, these updated chemical reactions are used to determine the changes in rock physical parameters and acid solution physical parameters, preparing for the initial variables and physical parameters for the next time step. 3. Results and discussions

This section investigates the influence of mineral and matrix heterogeneity on the acid stimulation process. Substantial research has been conducted on the acid stimulation of calcite. Comparatively limited studies have been devoted to acid stimulation within dolomite [4,28]. In nature, pure carbonate formations solely comprised of constituents like calcite or dolomite are rare. More commonly, carbonates are composed of a mixture of various minerals. This prevalent heterogeneity serves as a significant motivation for this work. Fig. 2 represents the key scenarios of mineral heterogeneity, encompassing uniform, layered, and heterogeneous distributions.

3.1. Effect of the components with uniform distribution

Fig. 3 investigates the scenario where solid minerals exhibit a uniform distribution. It is hypothesized that the carbonate rock is uniformly composed of two reactive components, calcite and dolomite. The figure summarizes the variation in the volume of acid solution required for a breakthrough in target core samples with different calcite contents under varying acid stimulation rates. Results show that acidizing breakthrough volume is sensitive to both acid injection rate and calcite content. The typical acid stimulation curve can be observed with different calcite content systems, in which more acid solution is consumed to achieve a breakthrough when the acid solution obtains very high or low injection rates. An optimum acid injection rate popularly exists in the acid stimulation systems with different calcite contents, corresponding to the minimum value of acidizing breakthrough volume. Calcite content affects the acid solution consumption efficiency and the optimum equilibrium condition. As the calcite content increases, the optimum acid injection rate rises while the minimum acidizing breakthrough value decreases. Fig. 4 presents a consolidated analysis of dissolution patterns formed after the acid stimulation process in carbonate rocks composed entirely of dolomite and pure calcite, which provides a boundary dissolution pattern for comparison. Typical dissolution patterns can also be observed corresponding to different injection rates, as shown in Fig. 4, including uniform dissolution pattern, ramified wormhole dissolution pattern, dominant wormhole dissolution pattern, conical wormhole dissolution pattern, and face dissolution pattern.
The acidizing process emerges from the interplay of convection, diffusion, and reaction, where these elements compete and cooperate. Compared to dolomite, the wormhole in calcite rock occurs at higher injection rates because faster convection transportation is required to balance the higher reaction rate. Hence, the acidizing curve is expected to shift to the lower right with increased calcite presence, as seen in Fig. 3. The curve disparity on the left side of Fig. 3 is caused by the difference in acid consumption between dolomite and calcite. With the same amount of acid and over the same duration, calcite experiences greater volume dissolution. Fewer pore volumes of acid solution are required to be injected to dissolve the same volume of calcite. Curves converge around at an acid injection rate of 1E−3cm3s−1 . Dolomite demonstrates a more effective wormhole-like penetrating dissolution pattern, whereas calcite exhibits a face dissolution-like conical pattern. The faster reaction rate of calcite is offset by the narrower, more rapidly penetrating dissolution of dolomite. Consequently, the optimum injection rate for dolomite results in a larger acidizing breakthrough volume compared to calcite because the slower reaction rate of dolomite allows for more acid effluent, thus increasing the acidizing breakthrough volume. The balance between convection and reaction initiates the advanced channels with more dissolution, causing the formation of wormholes. Multiple paths are activated at the beginning. One distinguishes through competition based on advantage accumulation. As the injection rate increases, the convection gets faster than the reaction. Acid breakthrough occurs at the wormhole boundary, resulting in a dispersed flow due to a smaller fluidity difference and a lower dissolution ratio. Dispersed flow patterns enhance acid consumption, delaying the breakthrough process. Finally, the slower reaction rate of dolomite leads to the earlier disappearance of wormholes. The flow emerges as approximately uniform.

Fig. 5 investigates the influence of non-reactive components distributed uniformly on the acid stimulation. Results show that on the low-injection rates, lower acidizing breakthrough volume values are observed for the larger presence of non-reactive components. This is attributed to the fact that the rocks exhibit face dissolution at these slow injection rates, and the presence of any non-reactive components means less acid dissipation and more penetration into the formation. As the injection rate increases and branching occurs, the effect of bypassing non-reactive components diminishes gradually until the optimum injection rate is reached. Wormhole formation is a mass-transfer-limited process, which means the acid dissipates efficiently with wormhole propagation. A dominant wormhole reaches the outlet for a breakthrough. Bypassing non-reactive components does not necessarily help the propagation of the dissolution front. Past the optimum injection rate, an opposite trend is observed for the presence of non-reactive components. The dissolution pattern becomes more branched since the acid solution is not dissipated fast enough when passing through the nonreactive components. The difference in the curves for dolomite and calcite further highlights the impact of reaction rate limitations on acidizing breakthrough volume. 3.2. Effect of the components with layered distribution

This investigation explores scenarios involving layered mineral distribution. Fig. 6 examines the cases in which reactive components are arranged in layers both horizontally and vertically. In each of these layered distributions, the contents of calcite and dolomite are kept at 50 % . Therefore,the calculations from the previous section,where uniform distribution had a calcite content of 50% ,serve as a reference solution for comparative analysis to discern the influence of layered reactive component distributions on the acid stimulation process. The calculations from the previous section, with a uniform distribution featuring 50 % content,serve as a baseline for comparative analysis to understand the impact of layered reactive component distributions on the acid stimulation process. The findings indicate that the distribution patterns of reactive components affect the acid stimulation process during relatively high injection rates. In the convection-limited region, the differences between both types of layered reactive component distributions and the reference solution are not significant. The vertical distribution has a more significant effect with increasing injection rates. Fig. 7 presents an overview of the dissolution images between the vertical distribution reactive component acid stimulation system and the reference acid stimulation system. At low injection rates, the dissolution patterns are similar, as well as the acidizing breakthrough volume values. When the injection rate increases, a clear distinction becomes obvious in dissolution patterns between calcite and dolomite. Calcite, being more reactive, results in higher porosity and less branched channels than dolomite.
Compared to the vertical layered distribution, the acidizing breakthrough volume variations are more complex for the reactive component with the horizontal distribution. Fig. 8 compiles the images of dissolution patterns formed upon breakthrough with horizontal distribution. As the injection rate increases, the dominant area changes from the below dolomite layer to the upper calcite layer, similar to the trends in cases with uniform mineral distribution. Within the balance region, flow generally favors the dolomite layer for the selected seed but shifts preference to the calcite layer at both extremities. At the lowest rate, due International Journal of Heat and Mass Transfer 227 (2024) 125560 to the dissolution being convection-limited, acid preferentially dissolves calcite, overcoming the initial preference. At intermediate injection rates, there is a pronounced preference for flow through dolomite regions, leading predominantly to dolomite dissolution. Unlike situations where flow favors calcite, a larger acidizing breakthrough volume is understandably observed in scenarios with horizontal distribution. The initial preferential flow in dolomite areas gradually diminishes as the acid penetrates deeper into the formation before reacting. A preference for flow gradually emerges in the more reactive calcite. As the flow becomes more uniform, the initial preferential flow is mitigated in the dolomite region. A critical observation is that without incorporating geostatistical considerations specific to mineralogy, the resulting curves can significantly diverge. If calcite initially exhibits preferential flow, there might not be any curve overlap, similar to the vertical scenario. This does not necessarily indicate a poor seed, but it underscores the significance of initial porosity values. In horizontal distribution, calcite and dolomite layers compete directly for fluid flow, unlike in vertical distribution, where such competition is less pronounced. When the number of layers is increased, as shown in Fig. 9, the acidizing curves increasingly resemble a uniform distribution, regardless of whether the distribution is horizontal or vertical.

3.3. Effect of the components with heterogeneous distribution

In addition to layered distribution, this section further delves into more heterogeneous distributions. The reactive components in focus consist of calcite and dolomite, which are heterogeneously distributed throughout the target core sample. The heterogeneity is categorized into two distinct aspects. GSLIB first generates the unique correlation characteristic of the reactive component. Subsequently, the magnitude of mineral heterogeneity quantifies the deviation of mineral content from the mean value. By pairing different correlation lengths and mineral heterogeneity magnitudes, boundary solutions are analyzed under three distinct scenarios: low heterogeneity with a correlation length of 0.01 and a heterogeneity magnitude (mineral) of 0.05 , medium heterogeneity with a correlation length of 1 and a heterogeneity magnitude (mineral) of 0.25 , and high heterogeneity with a correlation length of 100 and a heterogeneity magnitude (mineral) of 0.5 . Figs. 10 and 11, respectively, summarize the variations in acid breakthrough volume and dissolution patterns for each heterogeneity case.
Fig. 14. Effect of the porosity heterogeneity magnitude on the acidizing breakthrough volume: (a) Calcite rock; (b) Dolomite rock.
Fig. 15. Effect of the correlation length on the acidizing breakthrough volume: (a) Calcite rock; (b) Dolomite rock.
Results indicate that in the reaction-limited region, characterized by high injection rates, mineral heterogeneity creates optimal flow paths. Fluid flow through a homogeneous formation occurs uniformly, resembling a piston-like movement. Heterogeneous formations create pathways that favor preferential flow. At the lowest injection rate, formations with higher heterogeneity necessitate greater rock dissolution to achieve a breakthrough. This is likely caused by mineral heterogeneity, which compounds the overall heterogeneity originally attributed solely to porosity. As a result of increased heterogeneity, the dissolution front appears relatively flatter, leading to a higher acidizing breakthrough volume. In the balance region, nearly similar performance is observed between the low and high heterogeneity cases. At higher injection rates, the trend is inverted with higher acidizing breakthrough volume values for the lower heterogeneity case. When heterogeneity is low, the flow is more uniform. When heterogeneity is large, preferential flow paths are prevalent and allow for faster penetration of the dissolution front, hence lower acidizing breakthrough volume. At the optimum injection rate, no significant impact of heterogeneity magnitude is observed. Overall, the influence of heterogeneity distribution in components on acidizing among the studied range is limited, especially in the balance range.

3.4. Effect of the matrix with heterogeneous distribution

Drawing on research into mineral heterogeneity, this section further expands the study on the impact of matrix heterogeneity on the acid stimulation process. Different heterogeneity cases are constructed by varying the correlation length and porosity heterogeneity magnitude. Fig. 12 illustrates acidizing curves for low heterogeneity with a correlation length of 0.01 and a heterogeneity magnitude (porosity) of 0.1 , medium heterogeneity with a correlation length of 1 and a heterogeneity magnitude (porosity) of 0.15 , and high heterogeneity with a correlation length of 100 and a heterogeneity magnitude (porosity) of 0.2 . The results indicate that significantly different from the scenario with mineral heterogeneity, the volume of acid solution consumed by the breakthrough target core sample varies with the changes in matrix heterogeneity. Increasing the matrix heterogeneity in the system leads to a noticeable decrease in the acidizing breakthrough volume. However, the optimum acid injection rate for each different case of heterogeneity does not undergo significant changes. Fig. 13 shows calcite dissolution images for high and low porosity heterogeneity cases at different injection rates. The high heterogeneity images show larger areas of very low porosity, which indicates low or no contact with the acid. This increases efficiency and reduces the acidizing breakthrough volume. In addition, the high heterogeneity images show narrower wormhole formation, which allows for more efficient fluid propagation across the core. Decreasing the injection rate from a high injection rate towards the optimum injection rate leads to a decrease in the number and size of wormholes. The lower acid coverage and generally narrower wormholes observed in the high porosity heterogeneity case contribute to a higher optimum injection rate than the low porosity heterogeneity case.
To further investigate the reasons behind the significant variations in acid consumption volume caused by matrix heterogeneity, Figs. 14 and 15 explore the effects of different heterogeneity magnitudes and correlation lengths on the acid stimulation process. The results indicate that, compared to the heterogeneity magnitude changes, the acidizing breakthrough volume is more sensitive to changes in correlation length. Fig. 14 investigates variation in acidizing curves for calcite and dolomite by adjusting porosity heterogeneity magnitude. Higher acidizing breakthrough volume values are observed for low to mid injection rate values. This is due to enhanced branching within the ramified wormhole regime, increasing acidizing breakthrough volume. However, the effect is insignificant compared to the variations in Fig. 12, which shows that the impact of porosity heterogeneity magnitude alone has little effect on acidizing efficiency and does not significantly account for the previous trends.Fig. 15 shows the effect of correlation length on the acidizing curves for calcite and dolomite. For both cases, correlation length is shown to significantly impact acidizing efficiency. Increased correlation length results in lower acidizing breakthrough volume across all injection rates. This is caused by the presence of longer preferential flow paths extending horizontally across the core, which allows the fluid to flow more efficiently and spread less as the fluid front propagates. Hence, the effect of correlation length is more impactful on acidizing efficiency compared to that of heterogeneity magnitude.

4. Conclusions

Acid stimulation is widely used in carbonate reservoir development for its rapid efficacy and cost-effectiveness. Moreover, the phenomenon of wormhole formation, which is influenced by acid stimulation in conjunction with hydrological, thermal, and chemical processes, has also drawn significant research interest. This study is motivated to investigate the impact of rock property heterogeneity on the acid stimulation process. The two-scale continuum model is employed for numerical simulations, capturing mass, momentum, and energy transfer during the acid stimulation process. Moreover, the core-scale model is expanded to consider the specific composition of solid minerals, including reactive minerals such as calcite and dolomite and nonreactive ones like quartz and clay. A heterogeneity generator is developed by coupling with the open-source geostatistical program to produce different heterogeneous parameters using random seeds with varying spatial correlation lengths. The improved two-scale continuum model enables an investigation into the influence of mineral composition and matrix heterogeneity. The main conclusions are summarized below:
  1. The typical results of acid stimulation, which include the acidizing breakthrough volume curve and various dissolution patterns, are widely observed under different heterogeneity conditions.
  1. The optimum conditions for acid stimulation are sensitive to the concentration of reactive components. The optimum acid injection rate decreases with an increase in the content of dolomite. The corresponding optimum acidizing breakthrough volume decreases as the content of calcite increases.
  1. The mineral layered distribution can complicate the efficiency of the acid fluid used to stimulate the target core sample, especially in cases of horizontal distribution, due to the competitive consumption between calcite and dolomite. However, as the number of layers increases, the complex effect gradually diminishes.
  1. Compared to the heterogeneous distribution of minerals, matrix heterogeneity significantly affects the efficiency of acid fluid consumption. Increasing the matrix heterogeneity in the system leads to a noticeable decrease in the acidizing breakthrough volume. Matrix heterogeneity is primarily dominated by the correlation length.
The two-scale continuum model has garnered widespread attention for its high simulation accuracy and computational efficiency in modeling the acid stimulation process of carbonate rocks. In this work, the core-scale model is further expanded to consider the actual mineral composition of the carbonate rock solid matrix, allowing the existing model to simulate the acid stimulation process beyond the presence of a single reactive mineral. Additionally, a heterogeneity generation tool capable of considering geological autocorrelation heterogeneity has been developed to simulate more realistic geological conditions and expand discussions on the impact of core properties. Moreover, future research should leverage the current mathematical model to extend the scale of numerical simulation studies further, integrating them with reservoir-scale simulation processes. This would enable a more practical evaluation of the acid stimulation effects and guide the design of specific processes. Furthermore, the formation of wormholes after acid stimulation introduces new strong heterogeneity characteristics within the transformed area. The subsequent fluid preferentially flows through the low-resistance channels provided by wormholes. The impact of wormhole heterogeneity characteristics on the effectiveness of improved oil recovery methods and technologies, such as waterflooding, gas injection, and tertiary recovery techniques like polymer flooding, also requires investigation.
